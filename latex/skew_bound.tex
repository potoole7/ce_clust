Page 11 of \textit{Frank Nielsen, Two tales for a geometric Jensen–Shannon divergence} defines the extended skew-geometric Jensen–Shannon divergence with parameters $(\alpha,\beta)$ as
\[
\mathrm{GJS}_{\alpha,\beta}(p,q)
= (1-\beta)\alpha D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q) + \beta(1-\alpha) D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p) - B_{\alpha,\beta}(p,q),
\]
where $B_{\alpha,\beta}(p,q)=-\ln \int p^\alpha q^{1-\alpha}\ge 0$ is the Bhattacharyya-type term.

We let $\beta = 1/2$ since we're only interested in $\alpha$ (which we have as $\lambda$), therefore:
\[
\mathrm{GJS}_{\alpha}(p,q)
= \frac{1}{2}(1-\alpha)D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q) +\frac{1}{2}\alpha D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p)-B_\alpha(p,q).
\]

Since $B_\alpha(p,q)\ge 0$, it follows that
\[
\mathrm{GJS}_{\alpha}(p,q) \le \frac{1}{2}(1-\alpha)D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q) + \frac{1}{2}\alpha\,D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p).
\]

Both coefficients are nonnegative, hence
\[
\frac{1}{2}(1-\alpha)\,D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q) + \frac{1}{2}\alpha\,D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p)
\le \frac{1}{2} \max\{\alpha,1-\alpha\}\big(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p)\big),
\]
after factorisation.

By definition the Jeffrey's distance is $J(p,q)=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p)$, so we get
\[
\mathrm{GJS}_\alpha(p,q)\;\le\;\tfrac12\max\{\alpha,\,1-\alpha\}\,J(p,q).
\]
For $\alpha=\frac{1}{2}$ this gives $\mathrm{GJS}_{1/2}(p,q)\le \frac{1}{4} J(p,q)$ as before, while as $\alpha\to 0$ or $\alpha\to 1$ the factor tends to $\frac{1}{2}$.
Hence, for $\alpha = \frac{1}{2}$ we get the smallest divergence relative to the Jeffrey's distance. 
Intuitively, skewing increasingly causes the divergence to act more like the directional KL divergence that we weigh more, which is unbounded. 
Therefore, skewing and it's associated higher divergence can increase cluster separation, but may lead to numerical instabilities and reduce robustness to outliers, relative to choosing $\alpha = \frac{1}{2}$.
