\begin{abstract}
  % Intro
  Conditional extreme value models have proven useful for analysing the joint tail behaviour of random vectors. 
 % Conditional extreme value models have proven useful for analysing the joint tail behaviour of random variables. 
 These models describe the distribution of components of a random vector conditional on one exceeding a suitably high threshold, and have been shown to provide a flexible description of the distribution tails.
 However, one drawback of these methods is that model estimates tend to be highly uncertain due to the natural scarcity of extreme data. 
%  This motivates the development of clustering methods for this class of models; pooling similar within-cluster data drastically reduces parameter estimation uncertainty.
%  The development of clustering methods for this class of models is motivated by the need to identify the spatial structure of extremal dependence. 
 
 While there exists an extensive amount of work to estimate conditional extremes (CE) models in multivariate and spatial applications, the prospect of clustering models of this type has not yet been explored. 
 As a motivating example, we explore tail dependence of meteorological variables across multiple spatial locations and seek to identify sites which exhibit similar multivariate tail behaviour. 
 To this end, we introduce a clustering framework for CE models which provides a novel and principled, parametric methodology for summarising multivariate extremal dependence.
 
 % Outline of model
 We first define a novel dissimilarity measure for CE models based on the Jensen-Shannon divergence and common working assumptions made when fitting these models. 
 One key advantage of our measure is that it can be applied in any dimensions and, as opposed to existing methods for clustering multivariate extremal dependence, is not restricted to a bivariate setting. 
% Clustering is then performed by applying the k-medoids algorithm to our novel dissimilarity matrix, which collects the dissimilarity between all pairs of spatial sites. 
Clustering is then performed by applying the k-medoids algorithm on the dissimilarity measure, calculated for each pair of sites.  
 
 % Simulation
 A detailed simulation study shows our technique outperforms the leading competitor in the bivariate case across a range of extremal dependence structures and uniquely provides a tool for clustering in the multivariate extremal dependence setting. 
 \todo{May want to place below earlier, before sim?}
 We also outline a methodology for selecting the number of clusters to use in a given application.  Finally, we apply our clustering framework to Irish meteorological data, where we show how it can be used to identify sites with similar tail dependence structures between precipitation and wind speed.

\end{abstract}

\subsection{Asymptotic dependence} \label{subsec:asym_dep}

To characterise extremal dependence, we first introduce the concepts of asymptotic dependence and independence, as described in \citet{Coles1999}.
% The coefficient of asymptotic dependence, $\chi \in [0, 1]$, measures the degree of asymptotic dependence between two random variables $X_1$ and $X_2$ with corresponding CDFs $F_1$ and $F_2$, under the class of asymptotically dependent distributions.
The coefficient of asymptotic dependence, $\chi \in [0, 1]$, measures the degree of extremal dependence between two random variables $X_1$ and $X_2$ with corresponding distribution functions $F_1$ and $F_2$, and is given by % , under the class of asymptotically dependent distributions, and is given by 
% It is defined as $\chi = \lim_{u \rightarrow 1}{\chi(u)}$, where
% \[
% \chi(u) = 2 - \frac{\log\left(\mathbb{P}(F_1(X_1) < u, F_2(X_2) < u)\right)}{\log \left(\mathbb{P}(F_1(X_1) < u)\right)}.
% \]
\[
  \chi = \lim_{u\to1}\mathbb{P}\bigl[F_1(X_1)>u\mid F_2(X_2)>u\bigr].
\]
If $\chi = 0$, then $X_1$ and $X_2$ are said to be asymptotically independent while, for $\chi > 0$, they are termed asymptotically dependent, with greater strength as $\chi$ increases.
Thus, $\chi$ gives the limiting probability of $X_1$ being extreme given that $X_2$ is also extreme, and we expect the joint probability of observing extremal events in both variables to be greater when $\chi$ is higher \citep{Rohrbeck2021}.
Furthermore, $\chi$ is symmetric in $X_1$ and $X_2$.
% Note, two variables can be asymptotically dependent even if they are not dependent in the classical sense.
Note, two variables $X_1$ and $X_2$ can exhibit asymptotic dependence even when they are not dependent in the classical sense, i.e.\ they do not exhibit strong correlation in the bulk of their distributions.
% Another coefficient, $\bar{\chi} \in [-1, 1]$, is used to measure the strength of dependence in the case of asymptotically independent distributions, where $\chi = 0$.
Another coefficient, $\bar{\chi} \in [-1, 1]$, measures the tail dependence between two variables where $\chi = 0$, the case of asymptotically independent variables.
This coefficient is defined as $\bar{\chi} = \lim_{u \rightarrow 1}{\bar{\chi}(u)}$, where
\todo{Is there a better definition than this, given I've changed chi?}
\[
  \bar{\chi}(u) = \lim_{u \rightarrow 1} \frac{2\log\mathbb{P}(F_1(X_1) > u)}{ \log{\mathbb{P}(F_1(X_1) > u, F_2(X_2) > u)}} - 1.
\]
For asymptotic dependence, $\bar{\chi} = 1$.
Under asymptotic independence, $\bar{\chi} = 0$, with $\bar{\chi}$ increasing in magnitude as the variables become more positively (for $\bar{\chi} > 0$) or negatively (for $\bar{\chi} < 0$) dependent in their extremes \citep{Vignotto2021}.

Together, $(\chi, \bar{\chi})$ provide a useful summary of extremal dependence for any pair of random variables.

For example, probabilities of jointly extreme events for two or more variables cannot be calculated from these coefficients.

Now we have a metric over which to compute the dissimilarity matrix of pairwise ``distances'' between sites.
The choice remains as to which algorithm to use to cluster this matrix. 
As in \citet{Vignotto2021}, we use the Partitioning Around Medoids (PAM) algorithm, which is a variant of the popular K-medoids algorithm of \citet{Kaufman1987}.
A brief description of the algorithm is as follows:
    \todo{Have as algorithm block?}
    \begin{itemize} 
      \item The algorithm begins by randomly selecting $k$ medoids from the dataset as our cluster centroids. 
      \item Each observation is then assigned to the cluster of the nearest medoid, using the dissimilarity metric defined in \ref{subsec:div}.
      \item The centroids are updated by selecting the observation in each cluster which minimises the sum of dissimilarities to all other observations in that cluster.
      \item This process is repeated until convergence, when no observations change clusters.
    \end{itemize}
We choose this algorithm over alternatives such as k-means for several reasons. 
Firstly, since our clustering is designed to be performed in a spatial setting, it is preferable to have actual locations rather than summaries as cluster centres, for visualisation and also for refitting the CE model and reinterpreting results. 
Secondly, as we are clustering over a dissimilarity matrix defined over a functional space, we cannot assume that the clusters are spherical or equally sized, as k-means does. \todo{cite}
Furthermore, using the median instead of the mean to define the cluster centre is more robust to outliers, which is important in an extremal setting where data for some or many sites may be scarce. 

\subsubsection{Choice of k} \label{subsubsec:choose_k}

% Describe methods for choosing k (AIC, TWGSS) (silhouette not suitable)
The PAM algorithm takes a single hyperparameter, namely the number of clusters, $k$.
There is a rich body of literature on methods for selecting $k$, including the silhouette method of \citet{Rousseeuw1987} and the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) introduced in \citet{Akaike1974}.
However, the silhouette method is not suitable for our application, as it is designed for spherical clusters. \todo{Cite, have reference for this somewhere?}
The AIC is also unsuitable and has been found to perform poorly for our algorithm.
This may be because it requires aggregating over each location, which if unweighted assumes independence between the models fit for each site.
However, this assumption is often violated in spatial applications.
We revert, therefore, to the simple method of \citet{Thorndike1953-ie}, which uses the total within-cluster sum of squares (TWGSS) to select $k$. \todo{Fix citation?}
This method has been found to perform well in both our simulation study and our Irish meterological application, making clear suggestions as to the value of $k$ to use. 
Specifically, the TWGSS is defined as
    \begin{equation} \label{eq:twgss}
      % \mathrm{TWGSS}(k) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sum_{j=1}^{n_i} d_{ij}^2,
      \mathrm{TWGSS}(k) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sum_{j=1}^{n_i} d_{ij}^2,
    \end{equation}
    where $d_{ij}$ is the dissimilarity between site $j$ and the centroid of cluster $i$, which we measure using the $JS^{G_{\alpha}}$ divergence defined in section \ref{subsec:div}, and $n_i$ is the number of observations in cluster $i$.
We then plot $\mathrm{TWGSS}(k)$ vs.\ $k$, and choose $k^*$, the optimal number of clusters, as the value of $k$ at which the TWGSS reduction sharply slows, or equivalently, the point at which the curve has an ``elbow''.

\subsection{Mixture models} \label{subsec:sim_mixture}

In this section, we extend our simulation design to a mixture of Gaussian and t-copulas, where we control the dependence structure through their respective correlation parameters, $\rho_{\text{Gauss}}$ and $\rho_{t}$, with each t-copula having 3 degrees of freedom.
The idea behind this design is that the Gaussian copula generates observations exhibiting extremal independence, whereas the t-copula induces extremal dependence, the strength of which is determined by their respective correlation parameters.
While there are no theoretical guarantees about our estimates, we can still evaluate our clustering solution for this more difficult setting using the ARI.
Below, we will describe the results of this simulation study in a variety of scenarios, as defined by a grid search over possible $\rho_{\text{Gauss}}$ and $\rho_{t}$ values. 
In Section \ref{subsubsec:sim_competing_methods}, we compare our method to the leading competitor in the bivariate case, \cite{Vignotto2021}, and also show how our clustering algorithm uniquely provides a tool for clustering in the multivariate extremal dependence setting in  Section \ref{subsubsec:sim_extension}.
A more realistic setting is considered in Section \ref{subsubsec:sim_realistic}, where we simulate data from 60 locations with three unequally sized clusters, and perturb the correlation parameters to make the clustering solution more challenging.

We can use the bootstrapping scheme described in \cite{Heffernan2004} to determine the uncertainty in our parameter estimates. \todo{Do I need to describe this scheme and how it preserves the dependence structure of the data?}
As we are estimating two parameters, $\alpha$ and $\beta$, for which we do not know the true values, it may be that although our uncertainty is diminished post-clustering, the estimates may still be biased, with the parameters unidentifiable.
There are also many possible combinations of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ that could give the same conditional estimates, and so the estimates may be biased in this sense as well.

\section{Discussion} \label{sec:discuss}

% Rewrite Intro: Methods
In this paper, we introduced a novel method for clustering spatial locations based on their multivariate extremal dependence structure, as estimated by the conditional extremes model.
The $JS^{G_{\alpha}}$ divergence metric of \citet{Nielsen2019} is proposed as a dissimilarity measure to compare locations based on their estimated CE parameters, $\alpha$ and $\beta$, which together describe the strength and ``stochasticity'' of each set of bivariate extremal relationships. 
This method exploits the fact that inference based on the CE model follows a multivariate Gaussian distribution, following from the Gaussian assumption for the residual process.
This allows us to derive a closed form for the $JS^{G_{\alpha}}$ divergence, making it computationally efficient to use in clustering.
K-medoids clustering is performed on the dissimilarity matrix derived from this metric.
This preserves the spatial nature of the data and ensures that actual spatial locations with fitted CE models, as opposed to some aggregation, serve as cluster centroids. 
We can also choose to cluster on some or all of the bivariate extremal relationships in our data, and can aggregate information from multiple CE models to improve clustering performance.

% Simulation study
A simulation study was performed to evaluate the effectiveness of our method.
First, a bivariate Gaussian copula simulation was used to ensure that our clustering method works as desired, since the asymptotic values of the CE parameters are known for this copula.
Clustering solutions were perfectly identified, and parameter estimates showed both reduced uncertainty and bias when the CE model was re-fit post-clustering. 
We then extended this study to a more complex simulation design using a mixture of Gaussian and t-copulas, for a variety of simple and more complex dependence structures and scenarios.
We compared our method to the leading competitor in the bivariate case, \citet{Vignotto2021}, and showed that our method outperforms theirs across a range of possible bivariate dependence scenarios. 
Our method is also shown to be uniquely extendable to three dimensions and beyond, and providing more information to the clustering solution in the absence of additional noise is shown to improve clustering performance, as expected.

% Application
We applied our method to a dataset of Irish meteorological data, consisting of 59 locations with weekly rainfall and wind speed observations.
We estimated the CE model parameters for each location, and clustered the locations based on these parameters using our $JS^{G_{\alpha}}$ divergence-based method. 
Three clusters were identified, with the clustering solution showing distinct spatial patterns.
Namely, fairly well separated clusters representing the east and west coasts and the centre of Ireland emerged.
This was pleasing as no spatial information was used in fitting the CE model or clustering, and suggests that the CE model parameters are capturing some of the spatial heterogeneity inherent to the extremal dependence in our data. 

% Limitations: Gaussian assumption
There are several limitations to our method as currently implemented.
Our method relies heavily on the Gaussian assumption for residual process of CE model to provide a closed form for the $JS^{G_{\alpha}}$ divergence.
This may not hold in all cases, particularly for more complex dependence structures, and so the method may not be appropriate for all datasets.
For example, in \citet{Farrell2025}, the authors use the generalised and asymmetric generalised Gaussian distributions to model their residuals. 
While our closed form would not hold in this case, the $JS^{G_{\alpha}}$ divergence could still be used, but would require computationally expensive Monte Carlo sampling to estimate the dissimilarity matrix.
Indeed, it may be preferable to use the standard $JS$ divergence if choosing to forgo this Gaussian assumption, since it is bounded between 0 and $\log{(2)}$, while the $JS^{G_{\alpha}}$ is unbounded, as described in \citet{Thiagarajan2025}.

% Limitations: Uncertainty
The other important limitation of our method is in its treatment of uncertainty. 
We use the MLE estimates of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ to compute our dissimilarity matrix, and so the uncertainty in these estimates is not accounted for in the clustering solution.
This is less problematic in our simulation study where we often know the true values of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ and can perform extensive grid searches to ensure that the clustering solution is robust.
However, in real applications, the uncertainty in these estimates can be quite large, and so vastly different clustering solutions may be obtained depending on the estimated values of $\alpha$ and $\beta$.
To account for this uncertainty, one possible approach would be to use the bootstrapping scheme described in \citet{Heffernan2004} to generate uncertainty in the CE model parameter estimates, and then use these bootstrapped estimates to compute the dissimilarity matrix.
We could then produce a distribution of dissimilarity matrices, and use these to compute a distribution of clustering solutions.
This would allow us to account for the uncertainty in the CE model parameter estimates, and provide a more robust clustering solution.
However, this would be quite computationally expensive, and so would hurt one of the main advantages of our method, namely its computational efficiency, and may not be feasible for larger datasets.

% Limitations: Application
In this work, we focused on the methodological development and novelties of our method, and so we did not explore the application of our method to the Irish meteorological dataset in great detail, and so there are some limitations in this application.
  % \item Two important aspects of extremal modelling which we did not explore in detail were the choices of marginal model and dependence threshold. 
Starting with the marginal transformations, we used the ECDF to transform the data at each site to Laplace margins.
In the original \citet{Heffernan2004} model, the Generalised Pareto distribution (GPD) is used to model exceedances above an extremal threshold, combined with the ECDF for the ``bulk'' of data. 
This GPD marginal model can be extended to include covariates such as spatial location to inform the marginal distribution.
We wanted to show that our clustering could produce sensible results using just the ECDF in the early exploratory stages of modelling, where a sensible GPD or other such marginal model may not be immediately obvious.
We believe we were successful in this, showing that with no spatial information our clustering solution did indeed indicate some spatial structure in the extremal dependence in our data.
As future work, a more sophisticated marginal model could be considered, using the information we have gained from our exploratory clustering.

% Future Work: (Application, potential other uses)
There is scope for further work in this area, particularly in the application of our method to other datasets.
While we advertise our method as being uniquely applicable to any multivariate extremal dependence setting, our application is currently limited to bivariate extremal dependence.
Therefore, an obvious line of future work would be to extend this application to look at additional variables. 
The ERA5 reanalysis dataset, for example, where we get our wind speed data contains many variables that could be used in conjunction with our method, such as temperature, humidity, and pressure, all of which may have interesting extremal dependence relationships with rainfall and wind speed which could be explored.
We could also look at other datasets where multivariate extremal dependence is of interest.
